First Lego League

What's FLL?

FLL and its three elements (robot game, core values, and Project) create an intellectually stimulating experience that everyone can find an aspect to love. Every year, a new problem and robot game is introduced, and our goal is to create an innovation that solves the problem, a robot that scores the most points, and an engineering notebook while showing good sportsmanship.

Starting From the Bottom

My first experience with engineering, my 6th-grade Intro to Robotics class, allowed me to push my imagination to the brim. After seeing how passionate I was for robotics, my teacher, Ms. Rodrique suggested that I join one of her First Lego League teams. Since then, I have continued to become more passionate about robotics, and I have never looked back. When I circled Introduction to Robotics during course selection, I had no idea that it was going to lead me to the current passion that I have.

First Glimpse

Since I was a new to FLL, my first year was packed with information. I learned how important it was to plan your robot and code, how important simple code and attachments were, how important sensors are, ect… This year of FLL really showed me what was possible from such a simple game. In the end, we were awarded with inspiration award and got 3rd place in robot game at state competition.

Curious about our robot?

From Team Member to Team Leader

For my 2nd year of FLL (and my last) Ms. Rodrique asked if I wanted to create my own team, I accepted. I decided to form my team with two other co-captains (John Robert Walker and Madeline Brain), which allowed me to focus more on robot game.

As a team, we shaped how FLL teams at Armstrong Middle School competed. With a full engineering notebook, we set a new level of documentation for teams. With a fully printed board, we set a new standard of neatness.

I was in the robot room almost every day leading up to the state competition. There were times when I got angry at the hunk of plastic, that we called a robot but there were other times that I felt accomplished when the code ran consistently. This was my most successful year of FLL. We placed 1st in the robot game at state and regionals, which was never done before at Armstrong Middle School. In total, we got 3 awards (the Inspiration Award, the Innovative Solution Award, and the Robot Performance Award); We broke another record for the most awards given to a team in Mississippi FLL history.

Our Engineering Notebook

Our Legacy

The impact of our team is still seen today. This year I was asked to mentor the current FLL robotics teams in robot game. While I was mentoring, I noticed that our robot, Engineering Notebook, and Project Boards were saved to help guide future teams.

SeaPerch

What is SeaPerch?

Unlike most robotic competitions, SeaPerch is an underwater robotics competition. Teams create an ROV to navigate an Obstacle Course and to complete challenges on the Challenge Course.

Wait, you started a team?

Yes! I got tired of not having a robotics team for my 10th-grade year. Our competition is just a few months away. Wish us luck!

How hard can this be?

Pretty hard to be honest. With a team of 5 people, SeaPerch really put our creativity to the test. The more we tested our designs, the more problems we found. Very challenging!

But I loved it. It showed me what it was like Engineering in the real world. Every detail must be thought of in the design and it made me appreciate everyday items more. For example, why did Apple choose a 5.8-inch screen for the iPhone? Why not 6 inches?

Boy Scouts

Ew, the outdoors

Sticky, infested with bugs, and uncomfortable was what the outdoors were like to me as a Cub Scout. Now, because of scouting, the outdoors brings peace, comfort, and open air.

Keep an open mind

Cub Scouts to Boy Scouts

Transitioning from Cub Scouts to Boy Scouts caught me off guard. In Cub Scouts, each meeting was dedicated to signing off on a requirement. At the end of the year, I would be given a rank. Everything was planned out in Cub Scouts. In Boy Scouts, on the other hand, it was our responsibility to sign off on our own requirements. I was not prepared for this. It took me nearly a year to realize that the only way that I would be able to become an Eagle Scout was to work towards it myself.

My Leadership

As of right now, I am the Assistant Senior Patrol Leader (ASPL) for Troop 45, the second-highest-ranking youth leadership position in the troop. In the past, I was the Patrol Leader, and before that, I was the Assistant Patrol Leader of the Fang Patrol. All of these Leadership opportunities really shaped who I am, and allowed me to grow into the leader whom I would want to lead me.

9 years later

I'm closing in on the last few moments of the road to Eagle Scout, and I’m just now realizing how much Boy Scouts has transformed me. I used to be a child that depended on other people, but Scouts changed me into a persistent, independent, leader. I saw multiple friends of mine drop out of Boy Scouts due to the long grueling uphill to Eagle, and I was about quit Boy Scouts too. But I'm glad I didn't. I realized Eagle Scout isn’t what I wanted most in Boy Scouts; It was the memories and the new me that I wanted the most.

National History Day


National History Day is a competition for both middle and high school students. Every year a new topic is released. Our job is to create a project based on a historical event and how it relates to the theme of the year.

I severely undervalued history until NHD. For my first year of NHD, our topic was the thalidomide scandal, and this year, our topic is Radium Girls. We noticed that both of these topics have 1 thing in common. Money has control.
